# All of Us Research Program — Purpose & Status (as of July 2026)

*Compiled 2026-07-19 from NIH press release (Jun 30 2026), All of Us Research Hub data snapshots, Atlas of Longitudinal Datasets (May 2026), Wikipedia (Jun 2026), and Harvard Gazette (Jul 2026).*

## Purpose

The **All of Us Research Program** is a flagship **precision-medicine** initiative of the **U.S. National Institutes of Health (NIH)**. It was announced in the **2015 State of the Union Address** and launched for national enrollment in **May 2018**.

**Core goal:** build one of the largest, most diverse biomedical data resources in history to accelerate research and enable individualized prevention, treatment, and care. It aims to:
- Enroll **1 million+ U.S. adults (18+)** — deliberately prioritizing **diversity** (race, ethnicity, geography, sex, age, and people with rare diseases and underrepresented in biomedical research). Historically marginalized communities (e.g. Black Americans) are a specific recruitment focus, with community-engagement infrastructure (e.g. the Cobb Institute).
- Collect **multi-modal data per participant:** electronic health records (EHR), survey responses, physical measurements, wearables/device data, and **biospecimens** stored in a biobank (blood, urine, saliva).
- Generate **whole-genome sequencing** and broader -omics for hundreds of thousands of participants.
- Make de-identified data **freely available to researchers worldwide** via the **All of Us Research Hub** (researchers register, pass tier access, and analyze in-platform or via cloud).
- Reframe the participant relationship — participants are **partners**, with return of research results and a governance voice.

## Data types collected
- **Electronic health records** (EHR) — linked longitudinally
- **Genomic data** — whole-genome sequences (clinical-grade), genotyping
- **Survey data** — health, lifestyle, environment, family history
- **Physical measurements** — height, weight, blood pressure, etc.
- **Wearable / participant-generated data** (e.g. Fitbit)
- **Biospecimens** — biobanked for future -omics
- **Geolocation + demographic** data — enabling rurality/social-determinant studies

## Status (July 2026)

### 🏆 Major milestone — June 30, 2026 NIH announcement
All of Us is now the **largest integrated genomics + health database in the world.**
- **747,000+ participants' data** now available to scientists.
- Newest release includes **535,000+ whole-genome sequences** linked to **~482,000 electronic health records** — described as the most expansive data release in program history.

### Enrollment / recruitment (March 2026 baseline)
- **870,000+ participants enrolled** (consented).
- **560,000+ completed baseline data collection** (consent + EHR share + 3 surveys + physical measures + ≥1 biospecimen).
- Recruitment **ongoing toward the 1,000,000 goal**.

### Researcher uptake
- Data analyzed by **~2,300+ researchers** (per Wikipedia, Jun 2026).
- ~297,549 participants had genomic data early on; now >535K WGS — a >2× expansion in 2026 alone.
- Platforms: All of Us Research Hub (Researcher Workbench, cloud analysis), plus third-party tools (e.g. R2 genomics platform).

### 2026 program direction (GenomeWeb, Jan 15 2026)
- Plan **multi-omics data releases** through 2026.
- **New collaborations** and participant re-engagement.
- **Increase pediatric enrollment** (protocols for children/adolescents and cognitively impaired persons in development).
- Note: 2025 was marked by **budget cuts and a transition to a new participant platform** (Vibrent Health) — the 2026 releases represent recovery/re-acceleration.

## Relevance to this thread's research (spinal manipulation / chiropractic / rehab)
All of Us is increasingly used for **MSK, pain, and rehabilitation** research:
- Diverse EHR + genomic + wearable data enable studies of **low-back pain, chronic pain, and treatment response** at population scale.
- Harvard Gazette (Jul 2026) highlighted using All of Us EHR + genomic data for real-world, "messy" U.S. healthcare signals — directly applicable to pragmatic SMT/rehab outcomes research.
- It complements the **NIH BACPAC** consortium (Pitt/Utah/Duke SMT trials) by providing the large observational counterpart to RCTs.

## Caveats
- Enrollment still short of the 1M goal (~87% of the way on consented; ~56% on full baseline).
- Data is **observational/observational-linked** — not a replacement for RCTs (e.g. PACBACK); causal questions still need trials.
- EHR data is inherently fragmented/messy (per Harvard researchers) — requires careful harmonization.
- Budget uncertainty: 2025 cuts + 2026 presidential budget proposals targeted NIH; All of Us releases have continued but future scale depends on appropriations.
- Diversity is a stated strength but representation within some subgroups (e.g. pediatric, certain rare diseases) is still maturing.
